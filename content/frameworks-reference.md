## Good Strategy / Bad Strategy

**Author:** Richard Rumelt
**Source:** *Good Strategy/Bad Strategy: The Difference and Why It Matters* (Crown Business, 2011)
**Used in:** Both tracks -- hypothesis framing, channel strategy, 30-day planning

Most "strategy" is not strategy at all. It is a list of goals dressed up as a plan. Rumelt's insight: good strategy requires a hard choice about where to focus and why. It concentrates resources on the pivotal objective -- the one thing that, if done well, makes everything else easier.

Every good strategy has three parts (the Kernel):

1. **Diagnosis** -- Define the challenge. Identify the obstacle. Be ruthlessly honest about what is actually hard. A diagnosis simplifies reality by identifying certain aspects of the situation as critical. It is not a goal -- it is a clear-eyed description of the problem.

2. **Guiding Policy** -- The approach. How you will deal with the obstacle. The guiding policy channels action in certain directions without defining exactly what shall be done. It rules out a vast array of actions and thereby creates coherence.

3. **Coherent Actions** -- Specific steps that reinforce each other and execute the guiding policy. The power of strategy comes from mutual reinforcement -- not from doing many things, but from doing the right things together.

For startups with limited resources, concentration is survival. Winning one beachhead market creates the momentum to expand. Pursuing too many customers, channels, or features at once is the most common strategic failure.

## Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD)

**Author:** Clayton Christensen (with contributions from Bob Moesta and Tony Ulwick)
**Source:** *The Innovator's Dilemma* (Harvard Business Review Press, 1997); *Competing Against Luck* (Harper Business, 2016)
**Used in:** Growth Jam -- ICP definition, beachhead selection

People do not buy products -- they hire them to do a job. The "job" is the progress a person is trying to make in a particular circumstance. Understanding the job, the trigger event that prompts hiring, and the desired outcome reveals why customers actually choose one solution over another.

JTBD format: "When [trigger event], I want to [motivation/job], so I can [desired outcome]."

This framing shifts focus from demographic profiles ("35-year-old male, mid-market B2B") to behavioral triggers and motivations, which are far more predictive of purchase decisions. Two people with identical demographics can have completely different jobs. JTBD helps founders define their ICP around the situation that creates demand, not just who the customer is.

## The Four Steps to the Epiphany (Customer Development)

**Author:** Steve Blank
**Source:** *The Four Steps to the Epiphany: Successful Strategies for Products that Win* (S.G. Blank, 2005); *The Startup Owner's Manual* (with Bob Dorf, K&S Ranch, 2012)
**Used in:** Both tracks -- validation planning, hypothesis design, market analysis

Blank's core insight: startups fail not because they build bad products, but because they build products for customers who do not exist in the numbers assumed. The solution is customer development -- a parallel process to product development that tests assumptions about customers before scaling.

The four steps: (1) Customer Discovery -- find out if the problem and solution hypotheses are correct; (2) Customer Validation -- test whether you have a repeatable sales model; (3) Customer Creation -- build end-user demand; (4) Company Building -- transition from a learning organization to one focused on execution.

For workshop purposes, the key principle is that no business plan survives first contact with customers. Every hypothesis about ICP, pricing, channel, and offer must be tested with real people before resources are committed. The founder's job in the first 90 days is not to execute -- it is to learn fast enough to know what to execute.

## Double Diamond

**Author:** Design Council UK
**Source:** "The Double Diamond: A Universally Accepted Depiction of the Design Process" (Design Council, 2005; updated 2019)
**Used in:** Product Jam -- hypothesis engine (diverge/converge phases)

The Double Diamond is a visual model of the design process with four phases across two diamonds:

**First Diamond (Problem space):**
- **Discover** (diverge) -- explore the problem space broadly. Gather insights without judgment.
- **Define** (converge) -- synthesize discoveries into a clear problem statement. Eliminate what is not central.

**Second Diamond (Solution space):**
- **Develop** (diverge) -- generate many possible solutions. Prototype and test multiple directions.
- **Deliver** (converge) -- test, refine, and select the solution to move forward.

In the Founder Jam hypothesis engine, this maps to: diverge to surface 6-10 candidate hypotheses using thought starters, then converge to select the top 2-3 worth testing based on desirability, feasibility, and viability scoring. The discipline of diverging before converging prevents founders from anchoring on their first idea and missing better hypotheses.
